These conversations are boring and become repetitive over time. I'm more interested in the broader human-computer implications of our digital platforms for libraries and cultural heritage institutions. {#My technology choices in using Fedora 4 as linked-data store, is driven my preference for Python-based tool-chains in middleware and data storage with heavy focus on Javascript for end-user UIX. #}

Have you ever had a conversation with a friend or a stranger you just met about your work and you've had to explain what is a:

  • digital repository? (Duh, we're at the end of Open Repositories right!)
  • ILS?
  • Link resolver?
  • OPAC?
  • Discovery Layer?

You know what? Me too …

Instead, I think we need approach our broader challenges as libraries, museums, archives, and communities that care about our past, current, and future culture and information stores, through a compelling, narrative-driven, iterative technology I call a Catalog Pull Platform - an open-source vision of library technology that is owned and shared for us and by us to our institutions and people.

It may be shocking but people don't care!

What do people care about?

Maybe we shouldn't care (as much)?!

I do worry about library technology becoming even more homogeneous and insular then it already is, the hype and promise of the semantic web and library linked-data linked data in particular is that I shouldn't care about the production only the products between my systems and yours.
